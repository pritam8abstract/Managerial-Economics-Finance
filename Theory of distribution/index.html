What is the Theory of Distribution?

The theory of distribution is a foundational area of economic science dedicated to the systematic explanation of how a nation's total output, income, or wealth is shared among its population or the factors of production.

Core Questions

  • "Who gets what?"
  • "Why?"

Also referred to as the Theory of Factor Pricing, focusing on determining the prices paid for services rendered by factors of production:

  • Wages - price for labor services
  • Rent - price for land use
  • Interest - price for capital use
  • Profit - reward for entrepreneurship

The Two Faces of Distribution

Functional Distribution

The Macro Perspective

How total income is divided among factors of production:

  • Land ‚Üí Rent
  • Labor ‚Üí Wages
  • Capital ‚Üí Interest
  • Entrepreneurship ‚Üí Profit

Personal Distribution

The Micro Perspective

How total income is divided among individuals/households:

  • Income inequality
  • Wealth disparity
  • Social welfare concerns

üîë Key Insight

Classical economists like Adam Smith and David Ricardo viewed distribution as a partition among social classes. Ricardo called understanding these proportions "the principal problem of Political Economy".

Marginal Productivity Theory

Core Principle

Under competitive conditions, each factor of production is paid a price equal to its marginal contribution to output.

Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns

If one input increases while others are fixed, eventually additions yield progressively smaller increases in output.

Marginal Physical Product (MPP)

\[MPP = \frac{\Delta \text{Total Physical Product}}{\Delta \text{Variable Input}}\]

üìä Real-World Example: Indian Textile Factory

Fixed Input: 10 looms

Variable Input: Weavers

  • First weaver produces 50 meters/day (MPP = 50)
  • Second weaver increases total to 120 meters (MPP = 70)
  • Third weaver increases total to 180 meters (MPP = 60) ‚Üê Diminishing returns!

Valuing Factor Contribution

Value of Marginal Product (VMP)

Market value of additional output

\[VMP = MPP \times P\]

Where P = Price of Product

"What is the extra output worth to society?"

Marginal Revenue Product (MRP)

Change in firm's total revenue

\[MRP = MPP \times MR\]

Where MR = Marginal Revenue

"What is the extra output worth to the firm?"

Example Calculation (Indian Context)

If the 3rd weaver's MPP = 60 meters and each meter sells for ‚Çπ100:

VMP = 60 √ó ‚Çπ100 = ‚Çπ6,000

If the factory must lower price and MR = ‚Çπ85:

MRP = 60 √ó ‚Çπ85 = ‚Çπ5,100

Profit-Maximization Rule

Employment Decision Rule

\[MRP = MFC\]

Where MFC = Marginal Factor Cost

A firm hires additional units of a factor up to the point where the marginal revenue product equals the marginal factor cost.

üí° Decision Making (Indian Textile Example)

Using the textile factory example:

  • If weaver's MRP = ‚Çπ5,100 and daily wage = ‚Çπ500 ‚Üí HIRE (profit of ‚Çπ4,600)
  • If next weaver's MRP = ‚Çπ450 and wage = ‚Çπ500 ‚Üí DON'T HIRE (loss of ‚Çπ50)

Implication: A firm's MRP curve IS its demand curve for that factor. The downward-sloping MRP reflects diminishing returns.

Factor Pricing: Perfect Competition

Characteristics

  • Large number of firms
  • Homogeneous product
  • Price takers in both product and factor markets
  • P = MR (constant price)
  • VMP = MRP

Figure 2: Factor market equilibrium under perfect competition - W* = VMP = MRP

‚öñÔ∏è Allocative Efficiency

Workers are paid exactly the value of their marginal contribution to society: W = VMP

Factor Pricing: Monopoly

Characteristics

  • Single seller in product market
  • Price maker (faces downward-sloping demand)
  • MR < P (must lower price to sell more)
  • MRP < VMP

Figure 3: Monopolistic exploitation - Workers paid less than their value (W < VMP)

‚ö†Ô∏è Monopolistic Exploitation

Joan Robinson's term: Factor is paid W = MRP < VMP

Workers receive less than the value of their marginal product due to firm's market power.

Result: Lower employment than perfect competition.

Factor Pricing: Oligopoly

The Kinked Demand Curve Model

  • Few large firms with strategic interdependence
  • Rivals won't follow price increases (elastic demand above)
  • Rivals will match price cuts (inelastic demand below)
  • Creates "kink" in demand curve and vertical gap in MR curve

Figure 4: Kinked demand creates discontinuous MRP - explains wage rigidity

üìå Wage & Employment Rigidity

The vertical gap in MRP allows MFC (wage) to fluctuate within a range without changing optimal employment level.

Indian Example: Stable wages in industries like automobiles (Tata Motors, Mahindra) and telecommunications despite cost changes.

Kalecki's "Degree of Monopoly" Model

Core Argument

Distribution is determined by market structure and power, not just technology or productivity.

In imperfectly competitive industries, firms use markup pricing:

Price = Average Variable Cost + Markup (Œº)

Distributive Shares

Profit Share

\[\frac{\Pi}{Y} = \frac{\mu}{1 + \mu}\]
Wage Share

\[\frac{W}{Y} = \frac{1}{1 + \mu}\]

üîë Central Implication

Distribution is a power dynamic: Higher monopoly power ‚Üí larger markups ‚Üí higher profit share ‚Üí lower wage share

Indian Context: Corporate concentration in sectors like telecom (Jio, Airtel, Vi) and e-commerce affects income distribution.

Kaldor's Savings and Investment Model

Core Insight

Distribution determined by aggregate investment and differential savings behavior between capitalists and workers.

Key Assumption: Capitalists save more than workers (sp > sw)

Profit Share Equation

\[\frac{P}{Y} = \left(\frac{1}{s_p - s_w}\right) \frac{I}{Y} - \left(\frac{s_w}{s_p - s_w}\right)\]

üí∞ The Famous Aphorism

"Capitalists get what they spend (on investment),
and workers spend what they get (on consumption)."

Higher investment rate ‚Üí Higher profit share needed to generate required savings

Indian Relevance: India's push for infrastructure investment has implications for income distribution.

Non-Price Competition Impact

What is Non-Price Competition?

Competing through branding, advertising, quality, customer service, and innovation instead of price.

Two-Fold Impact on Factor Demand:

1. Level of Demand

Successful campaigns increase product demand ‚Üí raises price/MR ‚Üí increases MRP for production factors

Result: Hire more traditional workers

2. Composition of Demand

Creates demand for "creative class":

  • Marketing professionals
  • Data analysts
  • Designers
  • Engineers & scientists

Result: Structural shift in labor market

The Interplay: Linking Functional & Personal Distribution

The Classical View Has Blurred

Modern individuals derive income from multiple sources:

  • Salary (labor)
  • Rental income (land/capital)
  • Stock dividends (capital)

üîó The Critical Link: Asset Ownership

A shift in functional distribution (e.g., declining labor share) has indeterminate effects on personal inequality‚Äîit depends on who owns capital!

Scenario A: Concentrated Capital

If capital owned by wealthy elite:

‚Üì Labor share + Rising profits = ‚Üë‚Üë Inequality

Indian context: High wealth concentration among top 1%

Scenario B: Widespread Capital

If workers own capital (pensions, stocks):

‚Üì Labor share + Rising profits = ‚Üí Moderate inequality

Indian context: Growing mutual fund and stock market participation

"Homoploutia": High earners receiving substantial income from both labor (high salaries) AND capital (investments)

Measuring Personal Distribution

Lorenz Curve

Graphical representation of income distribution

  • X-axis: Cumulative % of population
  • Y-axis: Cumulative % of income
  • Line of Perfect Equality (45¬∞)
  • Actual distribution curve below

Gini Coefficient

Numerical measure derived from Lorenz Curve

  • Range: 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality)
  • Area between curves / Total area
  • Higher value = more inequality
  • India's Gini: ~0.35-0.48 (World Bank estimates)

Figure 7: Lorenz Curve for India - Greater bow indicates higher inequality

Synthesis & Key Takeaways

üéØ Central Themes

  • Functional vs. Personal: Bridged by asset ownership patterns
  • Marginal Productivity: Provides logic for factor demand (MRP = MFC)
  • Market Power Matters: Perfect competition ‚Üí fair pay; Imperfect ‚Üí exploitation
  • Macro Forces: Distribution shaped by monopoly power (Kalecki) and investment (Kaldor)

From Theory...

  • VMP = MRP (perfect competition)
  • VMP > MRP (monopoly/oligopoly)
  • W = MRP (hiring rule)
  • Diminishing returns drive demand

...To Policy (Indian Context)

  • Minimum wage laws (‚Çπ176/day)
  • Labor union role & reforms
  • Competition Commission of India
  • Progressive taxation & GST

Distribution theory is not mere academic exercise‚Äîit's an indispensable tool for comprehending and addressing the economic structures that shape modern society, particularly relevant for India's growing economy.
